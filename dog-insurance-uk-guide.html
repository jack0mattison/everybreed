Dog Insurance in the UK: What You Need to Know (2026)

EveryBreed · 2026

Veterinary care in the UK has never been better—but it has never been more expensive, either. A cruciate ligament repair can cost £2,000–£4,000, cancer treatment can run to £5,000 or more, and even a straightforward emergency consultation is rarely under £200. Dog insurance exists to protect you from these unexpected costs and to ensure your dog always gets the treatment it needs, regardless of your bank balance at the time.

Why insure your dog

The PDSA estimates that a dog's lifetime veterinary costs average £10,000–£17,000, and that figure rises significantly for breeds prone to hereditary conditions. Without insurance, a single serious illness or accident could force you to choose between your dog's health and your finances. Insurance gives you peace of mind and means your vet can recommend the best treatment, not just the cheapest. It's one of the most important ongoing costs of dog ownership, alongside food and routine healthcare. For a full breakdown of costs, see our puppy cost guide.

Types of cover

Accident-only is the cheapest option and covers injuries from accidents (such as broken bones or swallowing a foreign object) but not illnesses. It's better than nothing, but leaves significant gaps. Time-limited policies cover both accidents and illnesses, but only for a set period (usually 12 months) per condition. Once the time or money limit is reached, that condition is excluded from future claims. Lifetime (or covered-for-life) policies are the most comprehensive. They provide an annual benefit limit that resets each year at renewal, meaning ongoing conditions like arthritis or diabetes remain covered year after year as long as you renew without a break. Lifetime cover costs more, but it's widely regarded as the gold standard by vets and pet charities.

What's typically covered and excluded

  • Covered: vet fees for accidents and illnesses, surgery, medication, diagnostic tests (MRI, X-ray, blood work), hospitalisation, some policies include dental treatment, behavioural therapy, complementary therapies and third-party liability.
  • Excluded: pre-existing conditions, routine and preventative care (vaccinations, flea/worm treatments, neutering), pregnancy and breeding costs, cosmetic procedures and any condition arising within the initial waiting period (typically 14 days).
  • Excess: most policies have a fixed excess per claim (e.g. £75–£150) and some add a percentage co-payment, especially for older dogs.

Average premiums by breed size

Premiums vary based on breed, age, location and the level of cover chosen. As a rough guide for lifetime policies in 2026: small breeds such as French Bulldogs or Cavalier King Charles Spaniels typically cost £30–£60 per month, partly because both breeds are predisposed to certain health conditions. Medium breeds like Cockapoos or Whippets generally fall in the £20–£45 range—Whippets tend to be at the lower end thanks to relatively few hereditary problems. Large breeds such as Labradors or German Shepherds usually cost £35–£70 per month, reflecting higher vet bills due to their size and breed-specific conditions like hip dysplasia. Crossbreeds and mixed breeds generally attract lower premiums than pedigrees, though this isn't always the case.

How to choose a policy

Start by deciding on the level of cover: lifetime is recommended if your budget allows, especially for breeds with known health risks. Compare the annual benefit limit—£5,000 is a common entry point, but £7,500–£15,000 provides a more comfortable safety net. Check the excess structure (fixed, percentage, or both) and whether the excess increases with age. Read the policy wording for exclusions around dental cover, behavioural treatment and overseas travel. Use comparison sites to get quotes from multiple providers, but always read the full terms—the cheapest quote isn't always the best value. Finally, check reviews and claims experience; a policy is only as good as the insurer's willingness to pay out.

Pre-existing conditions and waiting periods

A pre-existing condition is anything your dog has been diagnosed with, shown symptoms of, or received treatment for before the policy start date. These are almost always excluded permanently. This is the single biggest reason to insure your dog as early as possible—ideally from the day you bring them home. Most policies have a waiting period of 14 days for illness and 48 hours for accidents, during which no claims can be made. Some conditions that appeared during a previous policy may also be classed as pre-existing if you switch insurer without continuous cover, so think carefully before changing providers. If your dog already has a condition and you're looking for cover, be upfront with insurers; failing to disclose can invalidate your entire policy.

Insurance might feel like an unnecessary expense when your dog is young and healthy, but the peace of mind it provides—and the financial protection when something goes wrong—makes it one of the smartest investments a dog owner can make. Arrange cover early, choose the right level for your breed, and review your policy at each renewal to make sure it still meets your needs.
