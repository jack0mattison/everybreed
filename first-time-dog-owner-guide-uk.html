First-Time Dog Owner? The Complete UK Checklist (2026)

EveryBreed · 2026

Bringing a dog home for the first time is exciting, but it can also feel overwhelming. There's a lot to organise before your new companion arrives, and the first few weeks set the tone for your entire relationship. This guide walks you through everything a UK first-time dog owner needs to know, from preparation to settling in.

Before you bring your dog home

Start by choosing the right breed for your lifestyle. Our breed-choosing guide and best dogs for first-time owners page can help. Puppy-proof your home by removing trailing cables, toxic houseplants (lilies, poinsettias, daffodils), small objects that could be swallowed and any chemicals stored at floor level. Set up a quiet space where your dog can retreat—a crate with a comfortable bed works well for puppies. If you have a garden, check the fencing for gaps; even small puppies can squeeze through surprisingly tight spaces.

Essential gear checklist

  • A well-fitted collar with an ID tag (a legal requirement in the UK under the Control of Dogs Order 1992)
  • A harness and lead (1.5–2 m, non-retractable for puppies)
  • Food and water bowls (stainless steel or ceramic)
  • Age-appropriate food recommended by your vet or breeder
  • A crate or bed in a quiet corner
  • Poo bags (biodegradable)
  • Puppy pads if you're bringing home a young pup
  • A few safe chew toys and a Kong for mental stimulation
  • Grooming basics: brush, nail clippers, dog shampoo
  • Enzymatic cleaner for inevitable accidents

Registering with a vet

Book a vet appointment within the first week. Your vet will check your dog's overall health, discuss vaccination schedules (or confirm what's already been done) and advise on flea, tick and worming treatments. In the UK, all dogs must be microchipped by law—if your puppy isn't already chipped, your vet can do this at the first visit. Ask about neutering timelines; most vets recommend discussing it once the dog reaches an appropriate age for its breed and size.

Insurance

Dog insurance is not legally required in the UK, but it's strongly recommended. A single vet bill for an emergency can easily run into thousands of pounds. Lifetime cover is generally the best option for comprehensive protection. Read our UK dog insurance guide for a full comparison of policy types and average premiums by breed. It's best to arrange insurance before you collect your dog so cover is in place from day one. Be aware that pre-existing conditions are typically excluded, so insuring early is important.

First-week survival tips

The first week is about building trust and establishing routine. Take a few days off work if possible. Keep things calm—resist the urge to invite everyone over to meet the new arrival straight away. Stick to a consistent schedule for feeding, toilet breaks and sleep. Puppies need to go outside immediately after waking, after eating and after play. At night, expect some whining; placing the crate near your bed can help your puppy feel less isolated. Be patient—house-training typically takes several weeks. Breeds like Labradors and Cockapoos tend to pick it up relatively quickly, but every dog is different.

Training basics

Start with the essentials: sit, stay, come and lead walking. Use positive reinforcement—reward-based training with treats and praise is widely recommended by UK veterinary and behaviour organisations. Keep sessions short (5–10 minutes for puppies) and end on a positive note. Socialisation is equally important: expose your dog to different people, environments, sounds and other animals in a controlled, positive way during the critical window of 3–14 weeks. Puppy classes run by accredited trainers (look for APDT or IMDT members) are an excellent investment. Breeds like Border Collies are highly trainable but need consistent mental stimulation, while more laid-back breeds like French Bulldogs can be a little more stubborn but respond well to patience and consistency.

Common first-time owner mistakes

The biggest mistake is choosing a breed based on looks rather than lifestyle fit. A Whippet might look delicate, but it's actually a low-maintenance, adaptable companion—while a fluffy breed that looks cuddly may need hours of grooming. Other common pitfalls include skipping socialisation, inconsistent training (one person enforces the rules while another lets the dog on the sofa), overfeeding treats, and not budgeting for ongoing costs. Our puppy cost guide breaks down what you can expect to spend in the first year and beyond. With the right preparation, your first year as a dog owner will be challenging but immensely rewarding.
