How Much Does a Puppy Cost in the UK? (2026 Prices)

EveryBreed · 2026

Buying a puppy in the UK involves more than the initial purchase price. From the cost of the puppy itself to ongoing food, insurance, vet bills and equipment, it pays to budget before you bring a dog home. This guide covers typical 2026 prices so you can plan ahead.

Upfront puppy prices in the UK

Puppy prices vary widely by breed, breeder reputation and demand. Kennel Club registered puppies from health-tested parents typically cost more but reduce the risk of inherited conditions and support responsible breeding.

  • Popular breeds (e.g. Labrador, Golden Retriever): roughly £1,000–£2,800
  • Designer crosses (e.g. Cockapoo, Labradoodle): often £1,200–£3,000+
  • French Bulldogs: often £2,000–£4,000+ due to breeding complexity
  • Rarer or working lines: can be higher

Very cheap adverts (e.g. under £500 for a pedigree breed) are a red flag. They often come from poor welfare backgrounds, with no health testing and higher risk of illness and behavioural issues.

First-year and ongoing costs

Beyond the puppy price, budget for:

  • Equipment: bed, crate, bowls, lead, collar, toys — £100–£300 initially
  • Vaccinations and parasite control: first year £150–£300+
  • Neutering/spaying: £150–£400+ depending on size and vet
  • Pet insurance: £30–£80+ per month depending on breed and cover
  • Food: £30–£80+ per month for an adult dog
  • Routine vet check-ups: £50–£80 per visit

Many owners spend £70–£200 per month on basics (food, insurance, preventative care) plus one-off costs. See our individual breed guides for cost-of-ownership sections (e.g. Labrador cost).

Insurance and vet bills

Pet insurance in the UK can cover unexpected illness and injury. Premiums vary by breed, age and postcode. Starting insurance when the dog is young usually keeps costs lower. Without insurance, a single emergency or chronic condition can run into thousands of pounds.

Hidden costs first-time owners forget

Puppy classes, dog walkers or day care if you work full-time, grooming for long-coated breeds, and replacing chewed items all add up. Factor in time off work for the first few weeks and potential behavioural support if needed.
