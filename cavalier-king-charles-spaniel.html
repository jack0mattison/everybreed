Cavalier King Charles Spaniel: Complete Breed Guide (2026)

SizeSmall Lifespan12–15 years ExerciseModerate GroomingModerate Good with kidsYes SheddingModerate

Overview

The Cavalier King Charles Spaniel has been the UK's most popular dog breed for decades, and for good reason. This friendly, outgoing gundog is equally at home in a family lounge or a muddy field. Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are known for their even temperament, loyalty and willingness to please, which makes them a favourite with first-time owners and experienced dog people alike. In the UK they are classified in the Kennel Club Gundog group and remain a top choice for assistance work, search and rescue, and as family pets.

Cavalier King Charles Spaniel temperament is typically gentle and sociable. They thrive on human company and regular exercise, and can become bored or destructive if left alone or under-exercised. Their double coat sheds year-round and more heavily in spring and autumn, so grooming and vacuuming are part of life with a Cavalier. UK owners should budget for quality food, preventative healthcare and insurance, as well as space and time for daily walks and play.

Whether you're considering a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel for a family with children, an active lifestyle or as a first dog, this guide covers size, lifespan, exercise needs, common health problems, grooming, training and the real cost of owning a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel in the UK.

Temperament & Personality

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are famously good-natured and people-oriented. They are typically friendly with strangers, other dogs and, when well socialised, with children. Their temperament makes them poor guard dogs but excellent family and working companions. Many Labs retain a playful, puppy-like enthusiasm well into adulthood and love fetch, swimming and interactive games.

Because they are so food-motivated and eager to please, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels usually respond well to positive reinforcement training. They can be boisterous when young and may knock over small children or fragile items, so supervision and training from an early age are important. Boredom or lack of exercise can lead to chewing, digging or barking, so providing enough mental and physical stimulation is key to a happy Cavalier King Charles Spaniel.

Size & Appearance

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are medium to large dogs. Kennel Club breed standards give approximate ranges: males typically stand 56–57 cm at the shoulder and weigh around 29–36 kg; females are slightly smaller at 55–56 cm and 25–32 kg. Build is athletic and sturdy, with a broad head, kind eyes and the breed's distinctive “otter” tail. Coat colours in the UK are black, yellow (from cream to fox-red) and liver/chocolate.

Exercise Needs

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels need a lot of exercise to stay physically and mentally healthy. Most adult Labs do best with at least 1–2 hours of activity per day: brisk walks, off-lead running, fetch, swimming or agility. Puppies and young adults have even more energy and need structured play and training as well as walks. In the UK, weather-resistant coats mean they can exercise in rain and cold, but avoid excessive exercise in very hot weather and provide shade and water.

Grooming Requirements

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels have a short, dense double coat that sheds throughout the year and more heavily during seasonal changes. Weekly brushing with a slicker or deshedding tool helps reduce loose hair and keeps the coat healthy. They don't need clipping or complex grooming, but nails, ears and teeth should be checked and maintained regularly. Baths are only needed when dirty or smelly; over-bathing can strip natural oils.

Health & Common Issues

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are generally robust but are prone to several breed-associated conditions. Responsible UK breeders screen for these where possible.

  • Hip dysplasia — malformation of the hip joint; can cause arthritis and lameness. Choose breeders who hip-score.
  • Elbow dysplasia — similar concept in the elbow; screening is available.
  • Obesity — Labs love food and easily gain weight, which worsens joint problems. Portion control and exercise are essential.
  • Progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) — inherited eye condition; DNA tests exist; buy from tested parents.
  • Exercise-induced collapse (EIC) — rare but seen in the breed; genetic testing is available.

Pet insurance in the UK is strongly recommended to cover unexpected vet costs. Regular check-ups, vaccinations and parasite control are part of responsible ownership.

Training & Intelligence

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels are highly intelligent and trainable. They excel in obedience, agility, gundog work and assistance roles. Start training and socialisation early: puppy classes, exposure to different people and environments, and consistent, reward-based methods work best. Their love of food makes them easy to motivate but also means they can scavenge or beg; teaching a solid “leave it” and good recall is important for off-lead safety.

Living Requirements

Cavalier King Charles Spaniels can live in a flat if they get enough daily exercise and mental stimulation, but they do best with access to a secure garden for toilet breaks and play. They are large, active dogs and can be clumsy indoors, so a house with a bit of space is ideal. They are not suited to being left alone for long periods; if you work full-time, consider doggy day care or a walker. UK climate is generally fine for Labs; they cope well with cold and damp but need shade and water in summer.

Cost of Ownership UK

Puppy prices for a Kennel Club registered Cavalier King Charles Spaniel from a health-tested breeder in the UK typically range from about £1,000 to £2,500 or more, depending on lineage and demand. Ongoing costs include: quality food (£40–£80 per month for an adult), insurance (£30–£60+ per month), preventative treatments (fleas, worms, vaccinations), vet check-ups and unexpected bills. Budget roughly £70–£150 per month for basics, plus one-off costs for equipment, training classes and any health issues. See our guide on how much a puppy costs in the UK for a full breakdown.

History & Origin

The Cavalier King Charles Spaniel originated in Newfoundland, Canada, where they were used by fishermen to retrieve nets and catch. They were brought to the UK in the 19th century and refined by British breeders for gundog work. The breed was recognised by the Kennel Club in 1903 and has been a fixture in British homes and fields ever since.

Is a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel Right for You?

A Cavalier King Charles Spaniel is a good fit if you can offer daily exercise, company, consistent training and space for a medium–large dog. They suit families with children (with supervision), active individuals and first-time owners who are committed to socialisation and exercise. They are not ideal if you want a low-shedding dog, have very limited time for walks, or need a quiet, low-energy companion. Compare the breed to others such as the Golden Retriever or English Springer Spaniel if you're weighing up gundog options.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average lifespan of a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel?

Most Cavalier King Charles Spaniels live between 11 and 13 years. Good diet, exercise, preventative healthcare and genetics (from health-tested parents) can support a long, healthy life.

Do Cavalier King Charles Spaniels shed a lot?

Yes. Cavalier King Charles Spaniels have a double coat and shed year-round, with heavier shedding in spring and autumn. Regular brushing helps manage loose hair.

Are Cavalier King Charles Spaniels good with children?

Generally yes, when well socialised and supervised. They are usually gentle and patient, but their size and enthusiasm can knock over small children, so training and supervision are important.

How much exercise does a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel need?

Adult Cavalier King Charles Spaniels typically need at least 1–2 hours of exercise per day, including walks, off-lead play and mental stimulation. Puppies need shorter, frequent sessions and no forced running.

What are common Cavalier King Charles Spaniel health problems?

Common issues include hip and elbow dysplasia, obesity, progressive retinal atrophy (PRA) and exercise-induced collapse. Buying from breeders who health-test helps reduce risk.

How much does a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel puppy cost in the UK?

From a responsible, health-tested breeder, expect to pay roughly £1,000–£2,500 for a Kennel Club registered Cavalier King Charles Spaniel puppy. Avoid very cheap adverts; they often come from poor welfare backgrounds.
