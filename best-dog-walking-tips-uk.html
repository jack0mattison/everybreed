Dog Walking Tips: How Much Exercise Does Your Dog Really Need?

EveryBreed · 2026

Regular exercise is one of the most important things you can give your dog. It keeps them physically fit, mentally stimulated and far less likely to develop destructive behaviours at home. Yet the amount of exercise a dog needs varies enormously by breed, age and health. This guide breaks it down so you can plan walks that truly suit your dog.

Why exercise matters

Dogs that don't get enough exercise are more prone to obesity, joint problems and anxiety-driven behaviours such as excessive barking, chewing and digging. On the other hand, the right amount of daily activity strengthens the cardiovascular system, maintains a healthy weight and provides the mental enrichment dogs crave. For working and sporting breeds in particular, a lack of stimulation can lead to serious behavioural issues that are often mistaken for stubbornness or aggression.

How much exercise by breed size

Small breeds such as French Bulldogs typically need around 30–60 minutes of walking per day. Their shorter legs and, in some cases, flat faces mean they tire more quickly. Short, steady walks twice a day are usually ideal. Medium breeds like Cockapoos generally thrive on 60–90 minutes of activity, split across two or three outings. They're energetic enough to enjoy a jog or a game of fetch but are usually content to settle afterwards. Large and high-energy breeds such as Labradors and Border Collies often need 90 minutes to two hours, with a mix of on-lead walking, free running and mental challenges. The Whippet is an interesting outlier—a medium-sized sighthound that loves short, fast sprints but is surprisingly calm at home and doesn't need marathon walks.

Off-lead vs on-lead

Off-lead time lets dogs run at their own pace, sniff freely and burn energy far more efficiently than on-lead walking alone. However, reliable recall is essential before you let a dog off the lead in public spaces. In the UK, many parks and commons allow off-lead exercise, but dogs must be kept on leads near livestock and on certain beaches during the summer. Start recall training early and practise in enclosed areas before progressing to open spaces. If your dog's recall isn't yet trustworthy, a long line (5–10 metres) is a safe compromise.

Seasonal tips for UK weather

Summer: Walk early in the morning or after 7 pm to avoid the heat. Pavements can burn paw pads—if the tarmac is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for your dog. Always carry water. Flat-faced breeds like French Bulldogs are especially vulnerable to heatstroke. Winter: Shorter daylight hours mean you'll often be walking in the dark, so reflective gear and a good LED collar are essential. Rinse paws after walks to remove road salt, which can irritate the skin. Rain: Most dogs cope well with rain, but towel-dry them thoroughly when you get home to prevent skin problems, especially in breeds with dense undercoats. A lightweight waterproof coat can help smaller or thin-coated breeds stay comfortable.

Signs of over-exercise and under-exercise

Over-exercised dogs may limp, lag behind on walks, sleep excessively or show stiffness when getting up. Puppies are particularly at risk because their joints are still developing—a general rule is five minutes of structured exercise per month of age, twice a day. Under-exercised dogs tend to gain weight, become hyperactive indoors, chew furniture or bark excessively. If you notice any of these patterns, adjust your routine and consult your vet if you're unsure.

Walking gear essentials

  • A well-fitted harness — reduces strain on the neck and gives you more control.
  • A sturdy lead (1.5–2 m) — avoid retractable leads near roads.
  • Poo bags — biodegradable options are widely available in UK supermarkets and pet shops.
  • A portable water bowl — collapsible silicone bowls weigh almost nothing.
  • Reflective gear — clip-on lights or a reflective harness for dark winter walks.
  • Treats — for recall training and rewarding good behaviour on the go.

Getting the right amount and type of exercise for your dog isn't complicated, but it does require paying attention to breed needs, weather conditions and your dog's individual signals. Start with the guidelines above, adjust as you learn your dog's preferences, and you'll both enjoy your daily walks far more.
